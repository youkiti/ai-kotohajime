# Unit B-2: Having the AI Ask Questions and Give Critique

**Related competencies**: B3 | **Estimated time**: 10 minutes

<div data-area-progress></div>

## Why This Skill Matters

Generative AI has two tendencies worth knowing about. One is that it **fills in missing information with its own guesses**, producing output that merely looks plausible. The other is that it tends to **respond favorably to the user** (it's prone to flattery). Left unaddressed, these tendencies leave you with "a deliverable built on mismatched assumptions" and "reassurance with no real basis." On the other hand, if you use instructions that **have the AI ask questions first** and instructions that **have it give unflattering critique**, the AI turns from a mere drafting assistant into a candid sparring partner that helps you prepare a project or presentation.

## Core Concepts

- **Technique 1: Have the AI ask questions (interview method)** — When you ask, "Before you create this, please ask me for the information you need," the AI will ask about the audience, purpose, and constraints. This is especially useful when you're not sure what to tell it in the first place. Capping the number of questions (for example, "up to 3 questions") makes them easier to answer.
- **Technique 2: Have the AI give unflattering critique (asking for critique)** — Show it your work as-is, and you tend to get praised. Specifying four things — a **role** (a strict reviewer or evaluator), an **explicit ban on flattery**, a **number of points**, and a request to **give reasons and suggested fixes** — draws out candid, concrete feedback.
- **Don't take the critique at face value** — Even AI feedback can include points that are off-base or mistaken. Judge the validity of each point yourself, and adopt only what you find convincing (the final call is always a human's job).

## Exercise Themes and Flow

Choose the tab closest to your own profession. There are three steps: **Step 1: Prepare something to be critiqued → Step 2: Have the AI ask questions → Step 3: Have the AI give critique.** You don't need a finished piece to critique — a simple draft, or the deliverable you created in [Unit B-1](b1.md), works fine.

=== "Physician"

    **Theme: Requesting critique of a conference presentation slide outline**

    **Step 1** — Prepare a draft slide outline for a case report or research presentation (bullet points are fine).

    **Step 2** — First, have the AI ask questions.

    ```text
    I'm going to present a case report at a regional conference. To make the
    slide structure as good as possible, please first ask me for the
    information you need. Please limit yourself to 3 questions.
    ```

    **Step 3** — After answering the questions, paste your outline and ask for critique.

    ```text
    Below is the slide outline I created.
    You are a strict attending physician with extensive experience mentoring
    conference presentations. Flattery and encouragement are not needed.
    For this outline, point out the 3 most important things to improve, in
    order of importance, each with "why it's a problem" and "how to fix it."

    (Paste your slide outline here)
    ```

## Steps

1. Prepare something to be critiqued (an outline, a plan, or a schedule). It doesn't need to be polished — a draft-stage version actually helps you appreciate the value of the critique more.
2. Send the **Step 2** prompt that has the AI ask questions, and answer the questions it asks. If a question makes you think, "I hadn't considered that," that in itself is a valuable outcome.
3. Send the **Step 3** critique prompt and receive the feedback.
4. Read the feedback and apply **only the points you find valid** to your deliverable. For points you're not convinced by, it's also good practice to push back with something like, "I don't think that point applies to my purpose here — why do you think that?"
5. **Keep this conversation history — do not delete it.** You can use it directly for the [Area B practical exercise](../quiz/b.md).

## Example of Expected Output

- In Step 2, you'll get questions that shape the direction of your deliverable, such as, "How many minutes is the presentation?" or "What does the audience look like?"
- In Step 3, you'll get concrete structural feedback, such as, "The conclusion slide doesn't answer the question you posed at the beginning," or "There's too much content for the audience's level." If all you get back is a typo check or "this is great," strengthen the role and condition specifications in your prompt.

## Common Pitfalls and How to Handle Them

- **Even after specifying a role, the AI still praises you**: Narrow the request further, for example, "Point out just the single most serious problem, without holding back," or have it assign a numeric score, for example, "Rate this out of 10 points and explain the reasons for any deductions."
- **The feedback is too abstract to know what to fix**: Ask it to "quote the relevant passage before pointing out the issue."
- **Too many questions to answer**: Limit them, for example, "up to 3 questions" or "one at a time."
- **It cites literature or guidelines of questionable authenticity as grounds for its critique**: Verify the existence of any cited source yourself (the hallucination you learned about in [Unit A-1](a1.md) can also occur during critique).

## Safety Note

!!! danger "Do not include patient information in the material you critique, either"
    Do not paste a slide outline or plan that still contains real patient information (including case details) or real individuals' names. If you're working with case report content, anonymize and abstract it before pasting. Follow your institution's rules for handling unpublished research data.
